### Summary

The colloquium combined logistical housekeeping and speaker introductions with five substantive presentations exploring AI's near-term and civilizational impacts. David Orban framed AGI as a civilizational-scale challenge requiring new coordination scaffolding; David Pearce focused on the philosophical and empirical problem of phenomenal binding and whether current architectures can host consciousness; Natasha Vita-More proposed a pragmatic adapt–discern–adopt framework for human–AI co-evolution and warned against fear-driven alignment paralysis; David Brin emphasized social context, individuated AI reputations, and existing societal tools for accountability; Gregory Stock highlighted how a generation raised with pervasive AI will experience cognitive extension, dependence, abundance, and shifting social bonds. Across talks, recurring concerns were institutional coordination, alignment narratives, empirically testable approaches to consciousness, and governance mechanisms such as reputation and auditing for AI systems.
