RaiseTalks proposed deal, cleaned summary

Context

The founder brought forward a proposed strategic partnership with a senior marketing operator she has known for about two years. She considers him one of the strongest founder-profile partners she could bring into the company, citing a prior exit that reached one billion users and his role in creating DAOs Hub. She sees his involvement as potentially decisive for RaiseTalks’ next phase.

Proposed partner contribution

The proposed package has three components.

First, $300,000 in cash.

Second, $150,000 in value-add resources and services, primarily related to marketing.

Third, a personal execution commitment to help RaiseTalks close its next financing round, described as a seed round of $1 million to $1.5 million, with a target company valuation of $10 million to $15 million by Q4 2026.

In addition, his expected operating contribution would extend into branding, UX, product positioning, website, strategy, marketing planning for 2026 to 2027, content strategy execution, support in hiring the right marketing lead, and access to a DAOs Hub package including local office-related benefits and membership.

Equity requested

The proposed equity package is structured as follows.

Stage 1, cash investment
 • $300,000 for 10% equity
 • This implies a $3 million valuation

Stage 2, services and strategic involvement
 • $150,000 in services/resources for 5% equity
 • This is framed as value-add plus personal involvement

Stage 3, financing KPI
 • If he helps the company close the next round, described as at least $1 million at a minimum $10 million valuation, he receives another 10% equity

Total ownership outcome

If all three stages are completed, his total ownership becomes:
 • 10% for cash
 • 5% for services/value-add
 • 10% for financing support KPI

Total: 25% ownership, with voting rights

That was the core point of clarification in the conversation. There was repeated confusion between raise size and valuation, but by the end both parties explicitly confirmed that the current plan, if fully successful, leads to 25% ownership for him.

Clarified stage table

Stage Consideration Equity Notes
Stage 1 $300,000 cash 10% Priced off implied $3M valuation
Stage 2 $150,000 services/resources 5% Marketing and strategic value-add
Stage 3 Help close $1M+ raise at $10M+ valuation 10% KPI-based equity, not necessarily capital from him
Total $300K cash + $150K services + financing support 25% Includes voting rights

Founder’s concern

The founder is broadly comfortable with the first 15%, meaning the combination of cash plus services, though not enthusiastic about the level of dilution.

Her real hesitation is around the additional 10% tied to helping close the next round. Her concern is that this may be an expensive price for fundraising assistance, especially because she believes she is already capable of raising capital and has momentum independently.

She is also thinking beyond the seed round. Her concern is not only economics, but long-term strategic alignment and the risk of granting too much ownership too early to someone who may later seek even more influence.

Specific question she asked

She floated a possible counterproposal: whether the additional 10% should vest over five years, rather than being earned immediately upon closing the financing KPI.

Advice given in the conversation

The advice given was essentially:
 • do not overcomplicate the structure
 • the 5% for services would already likely be long-term aligned if granted through an ESOP-style vesting structure
 • the $300,000 cash investment is itself long-term aligned, because its upside is only realized at liquidity
 • therefore, long-term alignment is already materially present
 • trying to impose five-year vesting on the KPI-based 10% may create friction, because in his mind that tranche is explicitly payment for helping close the next round

The recommendation given was therefore to accept the structure rather than renegotiate that specific point, on the theory that the overall strategic value and execution leverage justify it.

Analytical observations

1. The economics are not just “25% for $450K”

That framing is directionally useful but technically incomplete.

He is not merely buying equity at a blended price. He is being compensated across three distinct functions:
 • investor
 • operator / strategic contributor
 • fundraising catalyst

Those should ideally be priced separately, because they have different risk profiles and different verification mechanisms.

1. The most expensive tranche is the KPI tranche

The 10% for helping close the next round is the most structurally delicate piece.

Why? Because:
 • it is large
 • it is not clearly tied to cash from him
 • “helping close” is potentially ambiguous
 • it can create attribution disputes later

This is the part most likely to generate conflict unless the trigger condition is specified with precision.

1. “25% with voting rights” is a governance event, not just a compensation decision

At 25%, depending on jurisdiction, shareholder agreements, reserved matters, board structure, protective provisions, and future dilution mechanics, this may create de facto blocking power on certain matters.

So the real issue is not only dilution. It is control topology.

1. The founder is signaling two different preferences at once

She is saying:
 • execution matters more than prolonged fundraising
 • she is uneasy paying too much for fundraising help

Those are consistent, but they imply the deal should privilege operational value creation over a broad, loosely defined “help me raise” clause.

1. The conversation did not yet distinguish earned equity from purchased equity clearly enough

That distinction needs to be explicit in the actual deal paper.

Otherwise, future interpretation risk is high.

What should be clarified before documenting the deal

At minimum, these points need clean drafting:

First, whether the 5% services tranche vests over time, and on what schedule.

Second, the exact trigger for the 10% KPI tranche. For example:
 • amount raised
 • valuation floor
 • deadline
 • whether the capital must come from third parties, him, or either
 • whether introductions count, or only capital actually closed
 • whether partial success gives partial equity

Third, whether the 10% KPI tranche is issued immediately on close, or only after funds are received and settled.

Fourth, what “co-founder” means legally, if anything. In many cases it is symbolic language that creates expectation without legal precision.

Fifth, what voting rights, board rights, information rights, pre-emption rights, and protective rights attach to the stake.

Sixth, how IP created jointly is assigned, licensed, or owned.

My judgment

The transcript-level recommendation to “just take it” is understandable as practical advice under time pressure, but as a deal-structuring judgment it is under-specified.

The strongest version of this deal is probably not:
 • “25% if things go well”

It is closer to:
 • 10% purchased equity
 • 5% vesting service equity
 • up to 10% milestone equity, tied to a narrowly drafted financing and strategic-delivery condition

That preserves the strategic upside while reducing ambiguity and future dispute surface.
